The Promise

By the end of this workbook, you will have built a marketing system grounded in customer truth.

It will start with listening, extracting at scale what your customers actually value about your business, in their own words. This becomes the foundation everything else is built on.

It will reach people who don't know you yet, with content that addresses the real problems your customers told you they have.

It will capture their interest into a channel you own, so you stop renting attention from algorithms.

It will convert them into customers who pay premium prices, because the empathy you've demonstrated is built on what they actually said.

And it will measure all of it on a single scorecard you read every Monday morning, so you know what's working and what to change next.

Then you'll repeat the loop, improving each piece, until your average order value has grown so much that the business funds its own growth.

The Framework

Five phases. One loop.

The system has five phases. The first one, Listen, is the foundation everything else is built on. You'll work through them in order the first time, then loop back and improve each one continuously. Most of your business growth happens in the loop, not in the first pass.

The phases feed each other: what you discover in Listen shapes the content you produce in Reach, the content gets distributed through the channels you build in Capture, the relationships nurtured in Convert generate the data you analyze in Measure, and what you learn in Measure tells you what to refine across all the others, including running Listen again with sharper questions.

Every phase runs on two tracks at once. The first track is quick wins, the moves you ship this week that lift revenue inside six months. The second is compounding work, the system you build for the long run, the moat that pays for years. Most marketing teaches you one track or the other. This teaches both, simultaneously, in every phase. The quick wins fund and prove the compounding work; the compounding work makes the next round of quick wins easier.

And every cycle ends in a campaign. Six months is the typical first loop, long enough for the system to mature, short enough that founders feel the impact and can make a real commitment to the date. You pick the campaign. The work converges into it. Read on to the bottom of this section to write yours down.

The three layers of customer truth

Your customers know three things you need to extract:

  • What they tell you on the surface. Why they bought, what they'd change, whether they'd recommend. The standard feedback layer.
  • What they actually value beneath the surface. The feeling they're buying. The problem they're really solving. "I bought a watch" is surface. "I bought a watch because I just got promoted and wanted to mark the transition" is the real purchase.
  • The exact language they use. The words, phrases, and metaphors they deploy when describing your product, the problem it solves, and the experience of buying from you. This becomes the raw material for every piece of content and message downstream.
Proof Point, ALTHERR's Customer Truth
We've run our customer feedback survey continuously since 2024, 1,978 responses across two years. Four words dominate how customers describe ALTHERR: friendly (84%), competent (72%), professional (72%), personal (61%). Notably, "good value/preiswert" sits at only 23–28% and is declining. The brand is no longer perceived as a discount destination, and we know this because customers told us, in their own words, at scale.

Why statistical scale matters

Talking to 5 customers is anecdotal. You hear what the loudest ones think. The patterns that matter, the ones that should shape your marketing system, only emerge at 50, 100, 500 responses.

At scale, you start seeing things you'd never have guessed:

  • A discovery channel that's quietly become your top source (in ALTHERR's case, YouTube grew from 24% to 39% of new customer discovery over two years, visible only because we kept asking)
  • Specific words that come up again and again, across very different customers
  • Reasons people almost didn't buy, which are usually more diagnostic than the reasons they did
  • Differences between segments you assumed were homogeneous

Your goal is to extract the patterns at volume rather than read responses one by one, and that's exactly where AI agents earn their place.

Benchmark
Minimum useful sample: 50 responses. Solid sample: 200+. Continuous sampling (a few responses per week, every week) is more valuable than one big push. At ALTHERR, we collected ~800 responses per year, year after year, and could track how perceptions evolved.

What to ask

Your survey should be short enough that customers complete it (under 3 minutes), but structured to extract all three layers of truth. The categories that matter:

  • Discovery. How did you find us? (Reveals which marketing channels actually work.)
  • Brand association. What words describe us? (Reveals the brand language customers use.)
  • Service quality across dimensions. Rate us on the things that matter, and the same dimensions versus competition. (Reveals where you actually differentiate.)
  • Repeat intent and NPS. How likely are you to come back and to recommend us? (The two numbers that predict business health.)
  • Reasons for not buying. What made you hesitate, or what's stopped you from buying before? (Often the most diagnostic question on the survey.)
  • Demographics relevant to your business. Age, gender, and any segment markers that help you understand who you're serving.
  • Customer-specific data you can use later. The pet's name and birthday for a pet brand. The car model for an automotive brand. The watch reference for a watch retailer. Data that makes downstream personalization possible.
Proof Point, The YouTube Shift, Discovered in Data
In 2024, 26% of new ALTHERR customers said they found us through discount platforms. By 2026, that dropped to 19%. Meanwhile, YouTube went from 24% to 39%. This wasn't visible in marketing dashboards or revenue reports, it was visible in the survey, and it confirmed the strategic shift toward content-led acquisition was working. Without the survey, this critical insight would have been invisible.

The agent role in Listen

AI agents do two things here that humans can't easily do at scale:

  • Pattern extraction from open-ended responses. When 200 customers each write a paragraph about why they bought, an agent can read all 200 and surface the patterns, themes, and exact phrases that recur. A human would have to read everything, then forget half of what they read by the time they got to response #150.
  • Voice-of-customer mining across touchpoints. Beyond the survey, agents can read customer service tickets, online reviews, comments on competitor content, and social mentions. They surface the language and concerns appearing across thousands of touchpoints.

The output: a living document, the Customer Truth document, that contains the patterns, the language, the segments, and the benchmarks. This document becomes the source material for everything in the rest of the workbook.

You need the infrastructure to send the survey

A small but important point: to run this phase, you need at minimum the technical ability to send a survey to your existing customer base. If you don't have a way to reach customers via email or WhatsApp yet, the first concrete action in this phase is setting up that infrastructure, even just enough to run a one-time survey campaign.

This is a partial preview of the Capture phase. You'll build the full infrastructure later. For now, get the minimum required: a tool that can send an email or WhatsApp message to your customer list with a link to the survey.

The good news: doing this once gives you both the customer truth data and the start of the owned channel you'll need anyway.

Reach rewards presence with substance over raw volume. Your goal in this phase is to show up where your customer is already searching, asking, or scrolling, and to be so genuinely useful that they remember you.

The mistake most founders make is treating reach as an advertising problem. Ads can amplify, but they can't substitute for the underlying asset. If the content you're amplifying is thin, ads make you go broke faster.

The asset you need is expertise made visible: articles, videos, or social content that demonstrates you understand your customer's world deeply, using the topics and language your Customer Truth document revealed they actually care about. Done at the depth competitors won't match, because they don't think it's worth the work.

Competitors won't write the deep article about which dive watches actually perform in summer heat, the one that explains the difference between a Submariner, an Aquaracer, and a Seamaster for someone choosing their first sports watch. That's exactly why writing it is defensible.

What authority actually means online

Authority is the felt sense that this brand knows more about my situation than I do, and is willing to share what it knows generously, before I've bought anything. It has nothing to do with credentials or an "as featured in" badge.

Three signals build authority:

  • Depth. Articles longer and more specific than competitors are willing to write.
  • Specificity. Content that addresses real, named situations (a specific watch reference, a particular use case, a defined customer moment) rather than generic categories.
  • Founder presence. A real person standing behind the brand, visible enough that customers can trust who they're buying from.
Proof Point, The €0 Video That Worked
In 2021, we shot a YouTube video called "5 New Dive Watches in 2021", filmed in 25 minutes in the basement of my parents' house, in one take, with a single video producer. Today it has over 101,000 views, 6,487 hours of watch time (the equivalent of someone watching me for 0.74 years), 335 subscribers gained, and it still generates 2,200 views per year, four years after publication. The execution was 1%. The idea was 99%. That video taught us that format matters more than production quality: listicles became a fixed part of our content strategy, and they remain our highest-performing format across YouTube and the blog today.
Proof Point, Listen Confirms the Strategy
YouTube has grown from 24% to 39% of new customer discovery between 2024 and 2026, while discount-platform discovery has dropped from 26% to 19%. Content-led acquisition replaced price-led acquisition, and the customers told us so themselves, through the survey. The strategy was working; the data proved it.

The agentic content engine

What changed everything in the last 18 months: agents can now produce the depth-level content that used to require a full content team. Not generic AI-generated filler, actual expert-quality long-form, when you supply the brand voice, the topic intelligence, and the editorial judgment.

The engine has three components:

  • Topic intelligence, identifying the specific questions your customers are searching for, where competition is thin.
  • Pillar generation, producing 1,500–2,500 word articles per topic, grounded in your brand voice and product knowledge.
  • Derivative cascade, turning each pillar into Instagram carousels, reel scripts, ad creative variants, and email/WhatsApp content.

One strategic input becomes content across every channel you operate. Your role is to direct the work rather than produce it.

This is not hypothetical. On my own team, a content writer went from three SEO articles a week to fifteen to twenty by building exactly this engine: a workflow that holds the topic research, the brand voice, and the editorial checks, with him directing it. The output became scalable the moment he stopped producing every piece by hand and started owning the system that produced it. That is the same shift this workbook is walking you toward, applied to one person's week.

Make it sound like you, not like a machine

The agent produces content at volume. Whether that content builds trust or quietly erodes it comes down to one thing: voice. Most AI writing fails the same way. It is fluent, confident, and completely generic, so it reads like every other brand, which means it reads like no one. A premium customer feels that in a sentence, and it costs you the exact trust the rest of this system is built to earn.

Two inputs fix it.

First, a voice profile. A short document that captures how you actually write, so the agent has something specific to imitate. Capture five things: the lived experience that earns you the right to speak, your sentence rhythm, your formatting habits, the words you would never use, and three samples of your real writing. The agent reads this before it writes a single line.

Second, a banned list. These words and patterns mark writing as machine-made. Strip them on sight:

  • Hype and filler: leverage, unlock, unleash, empower, streamline, supercharge, maximize, optimize (when it is padding).
  • Consulting-deck adjectives: world-class, cutting-edge, best-in-class, game-changer, seamless, robust, holistic, scalable, innovative, disruptive, future-proof, synergy.
  • Marketing clichés: move the needle, low-hanging fruit, quick wins, win-win, thought leader, best practices, paradigm shift, value-add, actionable insights.
  • AI-tell words: delve, tapestry, realm, testament, navigate (as filler).
  • Filler openers and hedges: "In today's world", "When it comes to", "At the end of the day", "It's important to note that".

Then there are stylistic tells. Some patterns became so common in AI writing that they now signal machine-made even when a human types them. Decide which are genuinely yours, and ban the rest in your voice profile:

  • The contrast-negation reflex ("It is not X. It is Y."). It feels punchy, and it has become a dead giveaway. Write the positive claim directly and let it stand.
  • Stacked fragments used for rhythm ("Endurance. Grind. More posts."). Write the full sentence.
  • The em dash. The most overused mark in AI writing. A comma, a colon, a period, or parentheses will do the job.
  • One-line paragraphs as the whole structure. Used once, for a genuine beat of emphasis, they land. Used for every line, they read like a template. Keep them rare.

The rule underneath all of it: be specific, and sound like a person explaining something to a smart peer. Real numbers, named situations, plain words. If a sentence could have been written by any brand in any category, cut it and write the one only you could.

Make it look like you, not like a machine

Voice is half of it. The other half is how the work looks, and it fails the same way. DIY and AI visuals come out generic and inconsistent: a different font on every slide, a new colour each time, stock photos and clip-art icons that could belong to any brand in any category. A premium customer reads that as "they don't quite have it together," and it quietly costs you the same trust your voice worked to earn. The fix is the same shape as the voice fix: derive it once, codify it into the company brain, and let the engine apply it every time.

Two inputs fix it, the same two that fixed your voice.

First, a brand visual profile. A short document that captures how your brand looks, so the engine has something specific to apply. Start from what you already have. Most established brands carry some corporate identity already: a logo, a colour or two, brand fonts, sometimes a full brand book. Feed all of it in. Where it is thin, dated, or was never really yours, add a north-star, the one brand whose look makes you trust it on sight, the way you might point at Apple, and let it raise the bar. Out of both, you codify five things: one accent colour as an exact value, one typeface plus a web-safe fallback for email, a layout principle (generous whitespace, one idea per surface), your two modes (a light default and a dark cover mode for the moments that should feel premium), and the logo and any fixed assets the brand must always use. You build this in a single working session with Claude: hand over the brand book and the examples you admire, react to what it proposes, and walk out with the profile written. The engine then reads it before it renders a single slide, carousel, or cover.

Second, a visual banned list. The tells that mark a design as machine-made or generic. Strip them on sight:

  • Stock and clip-art: generic stock photography, clip-art icons, the three illustration styles every AI tool defaults to.
  • Decoration standing in for design: gradients, glows, drop shadows, badges, and accent lines under titles.
  • More than one accent colour: a second and third colour is how a brand starts to look like a sale flyer. Use one.
  • The default-SaaS look: the over-used blue on white, rounded everything, the three-icon feature row.
  • Shouting: ALL-CAPS HEADLINES, bold stacked on bold, exclamation marks, Title Case banners.

Then the stylistic tells, the visual equivalent of the patterns that betray AI writing. Decide which are genuinely yours, and ban the rest in your profile:

  • Heavy and black font weights for emphasis. Let size carry the weight. Semibold is usually enough.
  • Pure black text on pure white. A near-black on an off-white reads calmer and more expensive.
  • Crammed, centre-everything layouts. Whitespace is not empty space, it is the design. One idea per surface.

The rule underneath all of it: restraint, and one idea per surface. The test is simple, could this sit on your north-star's website without looking out of place? If any brand in any category could have produced the image, remake it as the one only you could.

Derive it the same way you derived your voice. For voice you started from real samples of your own writing. For visuals you start from your existing identity and a north-star, name what makes a look feel safe and premium, and codify that into your own system: your colour, your type, your spacing. Lock it once, and every deck, carousel, and cover the engine produces comes out unmistakably yours, the first time, with no designer in the loop.

Two audiences, two content streams

Most marketing courses teach content as if it's for one audience: strangers you're trying to acquire. That's half the picture, and the smaller half. Content has a second, equally important audience: existing customers, people who already bought from you, whom you're trying to bring back at higher tiers.

Each audience needs its own content stream, with different topics, formats, and tone:

  • Content for new customers is discovery-led. Topics are introductory and problem-focused, formats favor SEO and social algorithms (listicles, how-to guides, comparisons). The reader doesn't know you yet, the content has to be findable, instantly useful, and broad enough to capture them.
  • Content for existing customers is relationship-led. Topics are deeper, narrower, more advanced, they assume the customer already knows the basics. Formats favor owned channels (email, WhatsApp): founder POV, behind-the-scenes, customer stories, new arrivals framed against what they already bought, advanced topics for those who've moved up the ladder.

The agentic engine handles both, but the briefs are different, and so are the topic batteries. You'll build two of them: one for the strangers, one for the people who already know you. Same pillar can be reused for both, but reframed for each audience.

This is also why the six-month framing works. Content for existing customers compounds fast, you send today, they buy this week. Content for new customers compounds slowly, SEO ranks, audiences accumulate, trust builds over years. Both real, both running in parallel, each operating on its own timeline.

The third content type, social proof

Underneath both audience streams sits a third content type that quietly powers everything: your customers' own words about you. Testimonials, reviews, customer stories, survey quotes, distributed across every surface where prospects and existing customers encounter your brand.

Social proof matters because it does what your own marketing can't. You can say "we're trustworthy"; only your customers can prove it. Most marketing courses teach social proof as a tactic (collect reviews, slap them on a landing page). The high-leverage version is different:

  • It comes from Listen. The best testimonials are extracted, not written to order: they come straight from the open-ended responses in your customer survey, where customers were already telling you in their own words why they bought.
  • It's specific, not generic. "Great service" is worthless. The specific story, what they were nervous about, what changed their mind, who helped, what they noticed, is what converts.
  • It's distributed, not concentrated. A testimonials page is the lowest-value placement. Social proof embedded in context, next to the product, inside the email that's selling that product, in the ad creative for that exact use case, is where it converts. Place it everywhere: email, e-commerce, social, ads.

This is also where the agent layer earns its place. Mining hundreds of survey responses to find the 30 best testimonial-worthy quotes is exactly the kind of task that breaks humans and that agents do well at volume.

Proof Point, Social Proof at ALTHERR
Testimonials placed across email, e-commerce, ads, and social changed the trajectory of how customers engaged with the brand. The specificity of customer-authored stories, what they were nervous about, what closed the deal, who they spoke to, does what no amount of brand copy could. The 1,978 survey responses became the raw material: the open-ended answers were mined for the language and stories that now appear all over the customer experience.

What Didn't Work, Paid Influencer Marketing
Over six years, ALTHERR spent approximately €30,000 on paid influencer campaigns across Instagram and YouTube partnerships. The returns: essentially zero attributable sales. We salvaged some video footage that we still use today, but the commercial intent never converted. The pattern we discovered: when we approached influencers commercially, it bombed. When we approached the same influencers organically, appearing on their channels as a knowledge source, lending products for them to shoot with, positioning ourselves as the brand that brings authority to their content, the partnership compounded over years. The lesson: influencers respond to relationships, not transactions. If you're going to invest in influencers, invest in the long game. Pay-per-post is almost always a write-off.

Capture

Move attention from rented channels to ones you own, before the algorithm forgets you exist.

Quick Win · this month
Capture your existing customers into an owned channel.
Most businesses have hundreds or thousands of past customers with no captured contact. Get them onto email or WhatsApp now, send one well-crafted message, and you'll see repurchases inside two weeks.
Compounding · for years
Build the capture mechanic into every entry point.
Every article, every product page, every social touch routes interested prospects into the owned channel. The list grows weekly, compounds for years, becomes the moat the algorithm can't take from you.

Every minute you spend building an audience on Instagram, YouTube, or Google is a minute you're renting attention. When the algorithm changes, and it will, your reach evaporates. The companies that survive algorithm changes are the ones who systematically moved attention from rented channels into ones they own.

The owned channels are: email and, depending on your market, WhatsApp. In Brazil and most of Latin America, WhatsApp is now the primary direct channel. In Europe and the US, email still leads. In some businesses, both work in parallel.

Capture is where your funnel either compounds or leaks. Founders who skip this phase end up perpetually chasing new traffic. Founders who execute it well build a list asset that grows in value every year.

Proof Point
At ALTHERR, email subscribers have an average order value of €4,100, €300 higher than blended customers. Multiplied across thousands of orders annually, that delta is the entire ROI of running the email program. Email is a premium customer factory. And the reason it works: our Listen data shows customers consistently describe us with friendly (84%), competent (72%), professional (72%), personal (61%), the relationship layer we build through email is what makes those words apply.

Why WhatsApp in some markets, email in others

The right capture channel depends on your customer's behavior, not on what you prefer. In markets where WhatsApp is the daily communication default, Brazil, Mexico, India, much of MENA, WhatsApp open rates run 80–98%. Email, by contrast, runs 18–25% in Brazil.

In markets where email remains the default professional and consumer channel, Germany, the UK, the US, Northern Europe, email is the right primary capture mechanism.

The principle is the same: move attention to a channel you own, so you can keep the relationship going on your terms.

Tool recommendation: For email and e-commerce segmentation, Klaviyo is the right default, it's purpose-built for e-commerce, integrates deeply with Shopify, and has the segmentation logic premium brands need. For WhatsApp-first markets, layer a dedicated WhatsApp tool (Take Blip, Zenvia, or Wati) alongside Klaviyo. The two work in parallel: Klaviyo runs the email side, the WhatsApp tool runs the messaging side, and your customer data syncs between them.

Capture mechanics that actually work

The wrong way to capture: pop-up offering 10% off in exchange for an email. You get the email, but you've trained the customer to expect a discount on every purchase. Their AOV collapses.

The right way to capture: offer something genuinely valuable in exchange, a guide, a series, a community, exclusive content, that demonstrates the relationship you're proposing.

Three capture mechanics that consistently work:

  • Content gates: a deep guide ("The complete guide to choosing your first dive watch") gated by email signup.
  • Exclusive series: a 5-email expert series sent over two weeks, demonstrating your knowledge before any selling.
  • Community access: a WhatsApp group or invitation-only newsletter where customers get insights and previews.

Your owned channel is also a reach amplifier.

This is the insight most founders miss: once you've captured someone into email or WhatsApp, that channel becomes the primary distribution for the content you produce in Reach. Every pillar article, every video, every piece of expert content you create gets pushed to your owned list, keeping you top of mind continuously.

Practical implication: every article you publish in Reach should be accompanied by a planned send to your owned channel. Not a promotional email, the article itself, framed as the kind of expert content your subscribers signed up to receive.

The convert phase is where the relationship you've built turns into revenue. This is the phase that separates marketing programs that increase order volume from programs that increase average order value.

The mistake most ecommerce founders make: they treat email and WhatsApp as channels to push promotions. The customer learns to wait for the next discount. AOV collapses. The list becomes a liability, not an asset.

The right approach: every message in your owned channels should deepen the relationship before it asks for the transaction. Use the language Listen revealed your customers actually use. Address the feelings Listen revealed they actually buy. Then make an offer that's clearly worth more than what you're asking for.

This is where the customer-specific data you captured in Listen becomes operational. The pet's birthday becomes a birthday message. The watch reference becomes a "Speedmaster Professional is back in stock" notification. The car model becomes a service-anniversary reminder. Generic messages get ignored. Specific, personal messages get opened, and acted on.

And this is where social proof does its heaviest lifting. The single highest-leverage addition to any converting message is a relevant, specific quote from a customer who already bought what you're selling. A product page with three sharp customer stories outconverts one without them by margins so large it's almost embarrassing. Place social proof inside abandoned cart messages, post-purchase upsells, and product recommendations, not just on a testimonials page nobody reads.

Empathy at scale goes well beyond "Hi [first name]." It sounds like: "I remember you were drawn to the Speedmaster Professional last spring, the new Moonwatch with the Hesalite crystal just arrived, and I thought of you."

The four flows every business needs

Before you build anything custom, get these four automated flows working. They cover 80% of the conversion lift available to you.

  • Welcome series. The first 2–3 messages after capture. Set the tone, demonstrate authority, build the relationship before any selling.
  • Abandoned cart. The single highest-ROI message in commerce. In WhatsApp markets, this alone can lift conversion 3–5x vs email.
  • Post-purchase nurture. Order confirmation, shipping updates, and a check-in after delivery. The relationship deepens here, not at the point of sale.
  • Winback. Inactive customers re-engaged with content that reminds them why they bought from you in the first place. Often more valuable than acquiring new customers.

Where AI changes the game

Standard automation sends every customer in a segment the same message. AI-enabled automation reads each customer's full history and generates a message specific to them.

Three places where this changes conversion behavior:

  • Per-customer personalization at send time. An agent reads the customer's purchase history, browsing behavior, and current inventory state, then generates a message that references their specific relationship with the brand.
  • Inbound conversation intelligence. When a customer replies to a message, an agent classifies the intent and either drafts a contextual response for human approval or auto-responds for low-risk cases.
  • Campaign hypothesis generation. Weekly, an agent reads your scorecard and proposes specific campaign experiments based on patterns it sees in the data, surfacing revenue opportunities a human marketer would miss.

Here is what the first one looks like in practice. We watch our own data for a single signal: a customer who lands back on our site for the first time in six months, after going quiet. The moment it happens, the agent builds a message around what they missed while they were gone, the new arrivals and the most-read stories in the exact category they care about, and frames it as a welcome back rather than a sales blast. It is surgical, built on one person's behavior, and it only works because the owned channel and the customer data sit in the same place.

The reciprocity principle

I preach that decisions should be based on data. But underneath the numbers, the philosophy is simple: reciprocity. If I do something good for you, a piece of content that genuinely helps, a personalized message that demonstrates I know who you are, a recommendation I made even though it was the cheaper option, you feel a quiet sense of indebtedness. Not in a transactional way. In a relational way.

When the time comes for you to buy, that reciprocity becomes the reason you choose me over a competitor at the same price. Or pay slightly more, because the perceived value is higher. Or come back for the next purchase without comparison-shopping.

Every action in Convert should pass the reciprocity test: does this give first, before it asks? Emails that lead with value. WhatsApp messages that remember the customer. Offers that demonstrate you've been listening. Content sent to your list, not promotions blasted at them.

The second watch a customer buys with us tends to be more expensive than the first. The third more than the second. That's the ladder. It takes trust. It takes time. Every piece of content pays a little bit into the bank that justifies the next level.

The customer ladder

One of the most valuable patterns we discovered at ALTHERR: customers who stay engaged with our content over time progressively buy more expensive products. The first purchase might be €2,000. The second, €4,000. The third, €7,000. Eventually some customers reach €10,000, €25,000 purchases, but only after years of relationship.

This ladder doesn't happen automatically. It requires staying top-of-mind without being commercial. Every piece of content sent through the owned channel deposits a little trust in the bank. Years later, when the customer is ready for the next level, they choose you because you're the one who's been there the whole time.

In the watch industry specifically: 2% of customers make 98% of the revenue. The ultra-high-end purchases (Audemars Piguet, Patek Philippe, Rolex gold models) go to very few people. But the customers who get there don't buy a €25,000 gold watch from a stranger online. They buy it from the brand they've trusted for years, built one piece of content, one personal message at a time.

The principle extends beyond watches. Whatever your category, your most valuable customers are the ones who climb a ladder over time. Your job in Convert is to build the rungs.

Most founders are flying blind. They have dashboards in Shopify, in Meta Ads Manager, in Google Analytics, in their email tool, but no single place where they can see, in 30 seconds, whether marketing is working.

That single place is the scorecard. It's the most important tool in marketing and the most often skipped.

No trust is good. Proof and control is better. , The principle behind the scorecard

Where the scorecard came from

The scorecard wasn't a theoretical exercise. It was born from necessity in 2022, when I moved from Germany to Brazil and could no longer be operationally present at ALTHERR. The CEOs needed a way to see what their marketing team was actually doing. My team needed something to be accountable to. I needed proof that my work mattered from 10,000 kilometers away.

The scorecard solved all three problems at once. Every team member fills in their KPIs every Monday. Every Monday morning, we review it together. Green is celebrated. Red is brainstormed. Nobody hides. Nobody escapes accountability. Most importantly, everyone owns their numbers, which creates the kind of ownership culture that compounds over years.

Today, the scorecard does much more than measure. It's the operating rhythm of the team. People structure their week around their KPIs. They take pride in green weeks. They problem-solve red weeks together. That's worth far more than the numbers themselves.

The six numbers

These are the numbers that matter. Other metrics are useful for diagnosis, but these six are your weekly health check.

  • Total revenue, this week, last week, vs target.
  • Online revenue specifically, for businesses with physical retail.
  • Customer acquisition cost, blended across channels.
  • Owned channel revenue, what your email/WhatsApp list is contributing.
  • Reach indicator, organic traffic, content engagement, or whatever measures top-of-funnel.
  • Average order value, the number that proves your relationship is deepening.

That's it. Six numbers, every Monday, looked at for ten minutes before the week starts.

Proof Point
The scorecard does more than measure. It is the thing that lets you say no. This year ALTHERR's Google spend is running at a 69x return. So every new idea that wants budget, a postal mailing, a new platform, an influencer deal, has to clear the same bar before it scales: prove it brings back more than the number already on the page. That is the quiet power of the scorecard, it makes new spend earn its place instead of getting waved through on a hunch.

What gets removed from the scorecard

The hardest discipline in measurement is removing metrics that aren't useful, especially when they look impressive. Every vanity metric on your scorecard distracts from the ones that actually matter.

The single metric I removed from ALTHERR's scorecard that taught me the most: Instagram followers. Our CEO liked the number. It looked good next to competitor accounts. But the truth is brutal:

  • Followers don't see your content. The algorithm shows your posts to a fraction of them.
  • Followers can be bought. The follower count is fundamentally faked across most accounts.
  • Followers don't predict revenue. They don't even predict reach.

We replaced it with accounts reached in the last 30 days. That one metric tells the actual story: how many real humans saw what we published. It can be compared across channels (YouTube views, blog visitors, ad impressions). It can be compared against ad spend. It can't be gamed by buying followers.

That switch alone changed how the content team thought about their job. They stopped optimizing for follower count and started optimizing for content that the algorithm chose to push.

Audit your scorecard quarterly: what metrics are you tracking out of habit? What metrics flatter your ego but don't move the business? Remove them. The scorecard is sharpest when it only contains numbers that genuinely drive decisions.

Proof Point, The CEO Wanted the Vanity Metric
When I removed Instagram followers from the scorecard, the CEO pushed back, he liked comparing our follower count to competitors. I held the line because the metric was misleading. Sometimes the right call is removing what leadership likes to see, in favor of what actually matters. That's part of what the scorecard is for: forcing the conversation about what really moves the business, away from the comfortable surface-level numbers.
Proof Point
The scorecard at ALTHERR is what allowed me to see, month over month, that AOV was climbing while CAC stayed flat. That single insight, visible because the numbers were tracked weekly, was worth millions in compounded growth. Without the scorecard, the trend would have been invisible.

Why weekly, not daily or monthly

Daily is too noisy. You'll chase random fluctuations and make bad decisions. Monthly is too slow. You'll discover problems six weeks after they started.

Weekly is the rhythm that lets you see trends without being whipsawed by noise. It also matches how most founders actually operate, Monday morning planning, Friday afternoon review.

This is the highest-leverage agent in your entire stack. It transforms data into action without you having to interpret raw numbers every week.
