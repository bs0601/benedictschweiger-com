The grey market for pre-owned luxury watches publishes prices in real time. Any customer inquiring about a Rolex or Cartier at ALTHERR can open [Chrono24](https://www.chrono24.com) or [WatchFinder](https://www.watchfinder.com) and know exactly what the same reference trades for today. Traditional value-based pricing relies on information asymmetry. That asymmetry doesn't exist here.

In a strict pricing sense, ALTHERR is a price taker. The grey market is the floor.

And yet some customers — not most, but some — buy from ALTHERR at prices they could beat elsewhere. Drive 300 kilometres to pick up a watch in person. Come back again and again. Refer friends. Trade in watches and buy again.

Rainer is one of them. He's now spent over €20,000 with ALTHERR. He could have saved money on most of those transactions. He didn't. Understanding why is the most important thing ALTHERR has learned about value-based pricing — and it has nothing to do with the standard pricing playbook.

## The Standard Playbook Doesn't Work Here

Value-based pricing, as most pricing consultants define it, works like this: identify what your product is worth to the customer, quantify the value differential versus the alternative, set the price to capture a share of that value. It's a rigorous discipline. It works for software, for medical devices, for B2B contracts where switching costs are high and value is measurable.

It assumes you control the reference price. Or at least that the reference price isn't a click away.

In luxury goods retail, it's always a click away. The grey market is the alternative, and it's always open. Competing on value in the traditional sense means either beating grey market prices — margin destruction — or differentiating on something the grey market categorically cannot provide.

ALTHERR chose the second path. The question was: what does a specialist retailer offer that a grey market platform cannot?

## How Rainer Became a €20,000 Customer

Rainer found ALTHERR through YouTube. Specifically, through videos of Bene talking about watches — not advertising watches, but sharing the kind of knowledge that a serious collector actually wants: history, mechanics, market dynamics, how to identify the right reference for your taste and budget.

He watched tens of videos before he ever sent a message.

By the time he booked a consultation about a sale watch — a cheap watch, as it happened — he already had a relationship with ALTHERR. Not a transactional one. A personal one. When Bene called him, on time (a deliberate policy: in luxury sales, punctuality signals respect), Rainer was speaking to someone he'd been watching for months. The knowledge was familiar. The voice was familiar. The trust was already there.

He bought the cheap watch. That transaction wasn't the point.

In the years since, Rainer has spent more than €20,000 with ALTHERR. He's driven 300 kilometres to visit the store in person — not because he couldn't buy remotely, but because he wanted to. He's become an ambassador: commenting on live streams, posting about his purchases, acting as a testimonial without ever being asked. He trades in watches through ALTHERR, contributing to the certified pre-owned offering.

He could buy most of those watches cheaper. He doesn't.

This is value-based pricing. Not the Simon-Kucher version — the version that works in a completely transparent market.

The grey market can undercut a price. It cannot replace a relationship built through years of consistent, genuinely useful content.

## The Email List Makes the Same Argument at Scale

The Rainer case is qualitative. The email list data makes the same argument quantitatively.

ALTHERR tracks repeat purchase rates across its customer base. The overall repeat purchase rate for a new customer: 17%. Not bad for luxury goods retail, where average order values are high and purchase cycles are long.

For customers on the email list — receiving weekly content, never promotional, always orientated around genuine watch knowledge and inspiration — the repeat purchase rate is 34%.

The email list doubles repeat purchase rate.

The emails don't contain special deals or exclusive offers. They contain content: what's interesting in the market, historical context, guidance on what to look for. Value without the ask. The logic is the same as the Rainer case: consistent, non-commercial contact keeps ALTHERR present and trusted between transactions. When a customer is ready to buy again — or upgrade, or trade in — the relationship is already warm.

17% becomes 34%. In a market where you cannot meaningfully raise prices, that's the margin lever.

## What This Actually Means for Value-Based Pricing

The conventional model says: build a better product, communicate the value differential, price accordingly.

The ALTHERR model says: build a better relationship, communicate through content consistently, let the relationship do the pricing work.

In a transparent market, the second model is more durable than the first. Product differentiation in luxury goods retail is hard. Grey market competition erodes margin on any single transaction. But the grey market cannot compete with a relationship built through years of showing up — informing, inspiring, being present without asking for anything in return.

Rainer isn't loyal to ALTHERR because of product quality. The watches are available elsewhere. He's loyal because of what the relationship feels like: the trust, the expertise, the sense that the person on the other end of the phone actually knows him and cares about getting the right watch into his hands.

That feeling isn't a feature. It's a pricing mechanism.

## The Conversion Rate Behind the Philosophy

ALTHERR converts between 30 and 40 percent of inquiries generated through marketing. That number moves with brand and scarcity: a rare reference from a brand that's hard to source gives more pricing power and closes at higher rates. A commonly available watch on a market with steep grey market discounts requires flexibility.

But the baseline conversion rate — 30 to 40 percent — exists because the marketing isn't neutral. It's doing pre-sale work. Every YouTube video, every blog article, every email edition is building the willingness to pay before the customer ever picks up the phone. By the time they enquire, they're not doing a price comparison. They're confirming a decision they've already made emotionally.

This is the principle of reciprocity applied deliberately: give enough genuine value in advance that the customer feels a pull toward reciprocating. Not through guilt. Through trust.

## Building the System

The YouTube content that brought Rainer in was produced manually. Bene on camera, one video at a time. That production cadence doesn't scale indefinitely.

The next iteration of this system is agentic. The content engine — YouTube scripts, blog posts, email editions, social content — runs with far less manual input than the original YouTube channel required. The relationship-building mechanism is being systematised without losing the thing that makes it work: consistent, specific, non-salesy content that treats the audience as people who deserve real information, not marketing copy.

The email list data suggests this is worth systematising aggressively. A 2x difference in repeat purchase rate is not a marginal improvement. It is the pricing strategy.

If you're building a content engine and you're wondering whether the ROI justifies the effort, the ALTHERR data gives you one answer: the customers who receive your content buy again at twice the rate of those who don't.

That's not a soft branding metric. That's a hard commercial result.

## The Verdict

Most premium retailers treat content as a marketing cost — something you spend money on to drive traffic. ALTHERR treats it as a pricing mechanism: the thing that makes customers stop comparing prices.

The data supports the second model. Rainer proves it qualitatively. The 17% to 34% repeat purchase rate proves it at scale.

If you're running a premium business in a transparent market and you're asking how to justify a higher price, you may be asking the wrong question. The right question is how to build the relationship that makes the price comparison irrelevant.

Content is not a line item in your marketing budget. It's a margin driver. The distinction matters.
